Valavi R, Guillera-Arroita G, Lahoz-Monfort JJ, Elith J (2021). Predictive performance of presence-only species distribution models: a benchmark study with reproducible code. Ecological Monographs 0:e01486. https://doi.org/10.1002/ecm.1486.
This is a case study using a range of machine learning algorithms, including random forest and boosting (but not deep learning)

What general principles of machine learning algorithms are demonstrated among the models considered?
* bias-variance tradeoff (how complex is good enough to see complex patterns but not so complex to create high variance?) 
* controlling overfitting - don't just memorize training data and then fail on new data (use Lasso and MaxENT, e.g.)
* Ensemble methods - combining many weak models to try to build a strong model 

What modeling approaches were found to be best?
* the 6 highest performing models were BRT, RF down-sampled, MaxEnt, MaxEnt-tuned, MaxNet, and Ensemble
* they find that how you fit a model matters as much as which model you choose
* ensemble methods seem to outperform single models if well-tuned 
* no single best model though, depends on species and region and complexity 
* tuning matters enormously, sometimes more than the choice of algorithm itself!!

What is it about those algorithms that likely made them the best performers?
* They're all nonparametric, enabling them to tackle complex non linear relationships
* control for overfitting 

Can the performance of any of the algorithms be improved (and likely by how much)?
* yes, e.g. downsampling made the random forest model perform muc h better (from one of the worst to one of the best models)
* weighting helped SVM a ton too 
* careful tuning also helps, e.g. with Max-Ent 

Why does downsampled random forest perform better than regular random forest? Would this affect other algorithms considered in the paper?
* Regular RF sees far more background points than presences, so it learns to just predict "absent" most of the time
* downsampling helps with this by giving each tree an equal # of presences and backgrounds forcing the tree to learn what distinguishes the too rather than just learning from absences 

What could be the impact of the sizes of the datasets on the conclusions from this paper?
* some species had very few presence records (like 5) meaning that some models might overfit on such sparse data 
* also difference in survey sites between regions, e.g. <19,000 in NZ and only ~100 in AWT so model success may vary by region
